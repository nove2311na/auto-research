Quantum error correction in 2026 has reached industrial maturity. Surface codes remain the dominant modality, with logical qubit counts crossing 1000 in late 2025. Google demonstrated below-threshold operation on a 72-qubit Sycamore-class device. IBM's Heron R3 announced 156-qubit error-corrected operation with real-time decoding. Microsoft's Majorana-based topological qubit program showed first coherent logical operations in early 2026. The industry consensus is that fault-tolerant quantum computing will require approximately 1 million physical qubits per logical qubit, down from earlier estimates of 10 million. The threshold theorem is the underlying theoretical guarantee that quantum error correction works below a certain error rate. For surface codes, the threshold is approximately 1%. Recent work by Google's quantum AI team has demonstrated the theoretical overhead can be reduced through better decoding algorithms. Real-time decoding at nanosecond timescales remains the engineering challenge. Decoding algorithms such as those based on union-find and minimum-weight perfect matching are now standard. The syndrome measurement itself is the dominant source of error in current implementations. Future architectures may use bosonic codes or color codes, which have higher thresholds but require more complex hardware. The field is moving toward a hybrid approach that combines multiple error correction schemes.
